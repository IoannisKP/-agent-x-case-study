Agent X

Redesigning the new-player onboarding for Escape from Tarkov: a diegetic AI companion that activates after repeated failure, giving new players just enough guidance to reach their first extraction, without softening the game's hardcore identity.

01 · Background

The problem this project set out to solve

Escape from Tarkov is a hardcore survival shooter. You are dropped into a large map with no tutorial, no map, and one instruction: extract. Die or run out of time and you lose everything you brought and everything you found. The game teaches almost nothing in-game. New players learn from wikis, YouTube and Discord, or they quit.

The core problem

New players with limited time quit Tarkov early, because repeated death without understanding why feels like wasted time rather than progress.

02 · Vision & Objectives

What success looks like

Help time-limited new players feel progress and reach a first successful extraction, without flattening the brutal, high-stakes identity that makes Tarkov what it is.

OBJ 1

Keep players past the fork

Reduce early churn at the first playthroughs, where players decide to stay or quit.

OBJ 2

Just enough, not handholding

Give the minimum guidance needed to understand the core loop, not a full tutorial.

OBJ 3

Preserve immersion

Any help must live inside the game's world and tone; the first 3 raids stay untouched.

03 · Empathize · Research

Who the target group is

Not “new gamers.” The target group is experienced players actively seeking a harder, more punishing game. They don't want onboarding to make Tarkov easier. They want it to stop being needlessly confusing.

Prior hardcore-game experience is not enough to prepare you for Tarkov.
Ammo penetration and extraction are the most-cited hidden pain points.
The game is taught almost entirely through external resources, not in-game.
Every “click moment” was triggered externally, never by the game itself.

06 · Ideate · The Concept

Agent X

An in-world companion that activates only after repeated failure. Instead of a tutorial, the player receives an in-world message: Agent X will accompany the next raid and can assist. He guides to extraction, gives short tactical cues, and never breaks tone. Crucially, he can die, so even the help system carries Tarkov's stakes.

Diegetic

Lives inside the world. No tutorial overlay, no immersion break.

Earned

Appears after repeated failure, so it reads as a game event, not hand-holding.

At stake

Agent X is mortal. The guide itself can be lost. That is hardcore.

08 · Design Rationale

Interaction & information design

Interaction design

  • Activation is triggered by behaviour: Agent X appears after failed extraction attempts.
  • A binary in-world choice, “Let's go” or “I work alone”, keeps player agency intact.
  • Guidance is glanceable: a single HUD card, nothing that pauses the world.

Information design / UX writing

  • Copy is diegetic: Agent X speaks in-character and in Tarkov game-world flavour, never as a system prompt.
  • Cues are short and tactical: “Extraction is northeast. Move fast, stay low.”
  • Placed top-right, out of the sightline, so it informs without breaking immersion.

Graphic design is intentionally out of scope; this work was developed at wireframe fidelity.

09 · Test & Results

What the usability test revealed

3 think-aloud sessions on the wireframes. Testers were ages 40–46; none had played Tarkov.

Screen 3 · guidance

All three found the top-right cue without prompting and understood it immediately, notable given none knew the game. Strongest performer in testing. Keep as is.

Discoverable & actionable

Screen 2 · the offer

The post-failure appearance felt logical and the binary choice needed no explanation; all three chose “Let's go.” The interaction communicates on its own.

Read clearly

Screen 1 · death screen

Stopped no one, but “early termination” was misread as a technical error by one tester.

One misread

Screen 4 · the silence

None of the three noticed Agent X was gone, but the absence is meant to register through gaming experience this test group didn't have, so they could never have read it as intended. The result flags a real risk but can't confirm one.

Untestable with this group
10 · Recommendations & Next Steps

Where this goes next

Resolve Screen 4's ambiguity

The silent absence of Agent X couldn't be judged by this test group, since it depends on gaming experience the testers lacked. Test whether it reads for players matching the Seeker profile first. If not, a subtle in-world signal, e.g. an audio cue of Agent X being shot, could carry the meaning without breaking tone.

Keep Screens 2 & 3

The offer and the in-raid guidance are the strongest elements. No changes recommended.

Revisit death-screen copy

Reword “early termination,” which read as a system error to a new player.

Test the target audience

This round used players outside the primary “hardcore-curious” profile, which is why Screen 4 in particular couldn't be properly judged. A higher-fidelity round with players matching the Seeker profile, plus Tarkov veterans to judge tone, is the necessary next step to confirm whether the silence and the diegetic tone land as intended.

11 · Reflections

What the course taught me

What this course gave me was a new way of seeing products, digital ones especially.

Many years ago, a family member was in an accident that left him in a wheelchair. After that, I started noticing things I had walked past most of my life without a second thought: entrances with no ramp, walkways too narrow to pass through, a broken elevator, a door that won't open on its own. Once you see those barriers, you can't stop seeing them.

This course did the same thing to my eye for design. I now notice copy that confuses instead of explaining, CTAs that ask for a click without offering anything in return, interfaces that leave the user confused. But the deeper lesson was about perception itself, how differently people experience the same thing. I thought I already knew that. Then I sat down and interviewed users, and the gap between my assumptions and their reality became impossible to ignore.

That is what this project taught me in practice. I have never played Tarkov, though I know the genre and knowing the genre is not the same as knowing how a new player experiences the game. So I listened to them instead of assuming my own experience would match theirs. What I found was that my own assumptions, strong as they were, can get in the way.

The hardest barriers to see, it turns out, are the ones you carry in.
